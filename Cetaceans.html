Basilosaurus

One of the firsts "giant" predators that sadly got extinct too soon.

October 30, 2025 Belén López

Basilosaurus teeth reveal that its food was first chewed then swallowed. Scientists estimated the bite force of this cetacean by analyzing the scarred skull bones of another species of prehistoric whale, Dorudon, and concluded that it could have had a maximum bite force of at least 16,400 newtons and could possibly exceed 20,000 newtons.

This cetacean was one of the various species that suffered the massive extinction of the Permian-Triassic period, leaving no descendants and erasing its existence permanently, except for the fossils the species left. The mandibular canal is large and laterally flanked by a thin bony wall, the pan bone or acoustic fenestra. These features gave the ability to basilosaurs to hear directionally in water.

Livyatan

A giant cetacean who competed with the biggest shark in the sea.

October 30, 2025 Belén López

The fossil skull of the Livyatan had a curved base, which could have housed a large spermaceti organ. This organ helps modern sperm whales dive deeply to search for food. However, the Livyatan probably hunted large prey near the surface, so the organ might have had other functions. It could have been used for echolocation, sound displays, or even headbutting, possibly against rival males during mating or to strike prey.

It may have also been the top predator of its time, alongside the giant shark, the Megalodon, sharing the habitat with the Livyatan, and likely had a significant impact on the structure of Miocene marine communities.
